Abstract
 Large Language Models (LLMs) excel at generating syntactically correct text but remain limited in semantic understanding, leading to hallucinations, confabulations, and errors with potentially high-stakes consequences. EngLISP is a minimalist, Lisp-inspired reformulation of English designed to make the logical structure of thought explicit while remaining human- and machine-readable. By enforcing a Verb–Subject–Object order, class-based parentheses, and recursive nesting, EngLISP captures the natural hierarchy of ideas and encodes the Concrete–Abstract–Concrete′ progression of reasoning. When integrated into LLMs through corpus preprocessing, prompt translation, and output translation, EngLISP allows these models to operate at the semantic level, reducing hallucinations and improving reasoning without requiring a full system rewrite. This proposal presents EngLISP as a preliminary framework for enhancing semantic fidelity in LLMs, demonstrating its promise while outlining its philosophical grounding and potential applications in human and machine reasoning.

Introduction: The syntactic ceiling

LLMs have crossed an astonishing threshold: they write coherent prose, generate usable code, and summarize complex sources at scale. Yet their core mechanism — token-by-token prediction over vast text corpora — optimizes surface form. It is expert at how language looks, not what it means. The result is familiar: polished sentences that can conceal semantic errors, including hallucinations and confabulations that are hard to spot even for specialists.

Bigger models, larger datasets, and clever prompting help but do not erase the underlying gap. If we want reliability in high-stakes workflows (software, research, policy, education), we need a way for models to work on meaning, not just through syntax.

Claim: The fastest practical path to semantic gains is not a brand-new architecture, but a semantic intermediary that sits between human language and model tokens. That intermediary is EngLISP.

EngLISP: A minimalist language for recursive thought

EngLISP is an intermediate form — not a replacement for English, not a formal logic, and not a conlang for its own sake. It preserves English vocabulary while enforcing a small set of structural constraints that make meaning explicit and parsing unambiguous for both humans and machines.

Core principles (expressed narratively)

  • VSO order (Verb–Subject–Object). Every clause begins with an action, followed by who does it, then what it targets. This removes word-order ambiguity and provides a uniform spine for composition.
  • Class-based parentheses. Words/phrases carry explicit roles, e.g. (verb …), (noun …), (adj …). The functional skeleton becomes visible.
  • Recursion. Any expression may contain sub-expressions, nested without limit — mirroring actual thinking (claims justified by sub-claims supported by evidence).
  • C–A–C′ cadence. Reasoning often moves from Concrete observation to Abstract generalization and back to a new Concrete that incorporates the abstraction. EngLISP makes this dialectical motion first-class.

How EngLISP plugs into LLMs (no model rewrite required)

Keep your LLM’s token-by-token generator. Add EngLISP at three translation points so the model operates on semantics while presenting natural language to the user.

The three insertion points

  1. Corpus preprocessing
     Translate (and canonicalize) the training corpus into EngLISP. Canonicalization coerces different surface forms that share meaning into the same EngLISP representation (a MinimaLIST semantic normal form).
  2. Prompt translation
     On every user request, translate the prompt into EngLISP and canonicalize. Different phrasings map to the same semantic core.
  3. Output translation
     After the model generates in EngLISP, translate its output back to the user’s desired natural language. Users never have to see EngLISP — though expert users may input EngLISP directly to bypass translation.

Architecture with planning & refinement (your two-phase design)

  • Phase 1: Plan & expand. Layered “greedy” generators first draft a thesis → sub-theses → argument/evidence nodes (a tree that grows from outline to rough essay).
  • Phase 2: Revise & polish. Subsequent layers iteratively refine the draft until quality thresholds are met.
  • Guardrails: loop breakers (depth/width caps, quality gates) prevent infinite refinement.

EngLISP sits between layers as the lingua franca, keeping the evolving draft semantically stable while syntax gets reworked.

Why this helps: from syntax to semantics

Typical LLMs optimize for the likelihood of the next token, not the truth of the next claim. EngLISP forces the model to carry a meaningful internal scaffold: explicit actions, roles, dependencies, and nesting. Canonicalization collapses synonymous surface forms to a single representation, which:

  • Reduces spurious divergence across paraphrases.
  • Surfaces contradictions as structural conflicts instead of stylistic differences.
  • Improves editability (you can target and transform sub-trees deterministically).
  • Enables audits (you can trace where a conclusion arises in the tree).

Evaluation plan (what to measure, how to compare)

You don’t need a full compiler to begin measuring value. Start with a translator prototype and track:

  1. Semantic fidelity: agreement with gold truth sets under paraphrase stress tests (same meaning, different surface form).
  2. Contradiction rate: internal consistency in long outputs (fewer latent self-contradictions).
  3. Hallucination hit-rate: fact-checking audits (precision/recall on factual claims).
  4. Task reliability: downstream correctness on code, math, and reasoning benchmarks when inputs are paraphrased.
  5. Calibration: probability/hedging alignment with correctness (overconfident wrong answers should drop).

Design A/B experiments comparing baseline LLM vs LLM+EngLISP across identical prompts.

Anticipated responses (and direct answers)

  • “Is this just a conlang or a Lisp syntax cosplay?”
     No. EngLISP is an operational intermediary built to preserve meaning and expose structure for humans and machines. The Lisp resemblance is purposeful: recursion and parenthesized trees are a proven way to represent nested structure compactly.
  • “Why not Prolog/first-order logic/type theory?”
     Those are excellent for formal reasoning but poor at everyday discourse and philosophical narrative. EngLISP preserves English vocabulary while enforcing just enough structure for parsing and composition.
  • “Do I need a full compiler to see benefits?”
     No. A translator with canonicalization is enough to (1) stabilize semantics across paraphrases, (2) reduce contradictions, and (3) make edits/audits tractable — today.

Philosophical significance (in brief)

EngLISP encodes the Concrete → Abstract → Concrete′ movement as a first-class syntactic rhythm. This mirrors classical dialectics (e.g., Hegel’s Becoming) and illuminates how concepts arise from practice and return to transform it. It also offers a practical lens on mystification: you can trace how real relations become abstracted and reappear as inverted ideals — because the path is explicit in the syntax.

Limitations and open questions

  • Translation cost: latency/compute overhead for NL↔EngLISP is non-zero; performance depends on grammar coverage and implementation.
  • Coverage vs. minimalism: aggressive minimalism is a feature; scope creep must be managed so EngLISP remains lean and teachable.
  • Human ergonomics: most users will prefer natural language; EngLISP is primarily an internal semantic layer (with an optional expert interface).

Conclusion

If LLMs are to be trusted in workflows where small mistakes have big consequences, they must reason over meaning, not just through syntax. EngLISP is a practical way to make that shift today. By enforcing a tiny set of structural commitments — VSO order, role tags, recursion, and a C–A–C′ cadence — and inserting a translator at three pipeline points, we can stabilize semantics, reduce hallucinations, and make long-form reasoning auditable and editable. Minimal, explicit, recursive — and ready to prototype.
