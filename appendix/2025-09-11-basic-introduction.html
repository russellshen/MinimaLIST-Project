I created the MinimaLIST heuristic that condenses complex concrete domains into the set of abstractions minimally sufficient to explain its causal essence, rendering its domain simple and clear once it maps out and explains the rest of the domain from the choice of the set of abstractions, which I also call a MinimaLIST.

The tradeoff between achieving minimalism and sufficiency maps out a Pareto frontier, but this appears as a different curve for different questions and academic disciplines. For each, it takes on a different context, first principles, and idiomatic form, but the general heuristic principle is held invariant between its concrete applications.

I claim that the process of deliberating upon possible choices of abstractions for the domain, eliminating as much of the topic space as possible as reducible to a more central basic unit, choosing the best set of truly indispensable building blocks found thus far given one’s definitions of minimalism and sufficiency within this context, and branching out from this minimally sufficient set of abstractions, namely the MinimaLIST, as the “signal” or as what is primary and central to explain the rest of the domain as incidental arbitrary “noise” or secondary dependencies is better than any other heuristic rule for most, if not all, domains.

The mode of inquiry is always different from the mode of presentation and in many respects it is the opposite, with the former being a movement from the chaotic opaque Concrete to the Abstract, and the latter being a movement from the Abstract to the Concrete’, i.e., a new Concrete consolidated upon the novel basis established through the formation of the Abstract, tracing from the MinimaLIST outwards until it can explain what is outside of it in its topic scope using its own immanent logic. The MinimaLIST is the tiniest seed that can sprout to reproduce the full Concrete and once we know that, we can follow the seed’s shoots to see how everything else relates to it.

The movement of genuine scientific reasoning is always this way, Concrete-Abstract-Concrete’ (or C-A-C’), first collapse then expand, and I am not claiming to have invented this interpretation of human cognition, for which we have much to learn from Hegel, Marx, and Engels. The nearly-universal MinimaLIST heuristic for the A in C-A-C’ is specifically to deploy minimally sufficient sets of abstractions and thereby achieve far more understandable, lightweight, and accurate models than any other means of traversing the categorically true C-A-C’ schematic, i.e., bloated redundant inconsistent unimaginative sets of atomic units chained together with disastrously loose bad-faith mystical esoteric idiosyncratic logical inferences that spread distortion, error, and intellectual dishonesty everywhere they propagate.

Using the MinimaLIST method, I created the EngLISP intermediate language for a minimal sufficient coerced canonical semantic natural language representation into which LLMs can translate natural language text corpuses and user prompts, a standardized formalization of English lexicography within Lisp homoiconic S-expressions so that EngLISP can be read both as natural language sentences for all humans and be valid Lisp programs that can be compiled or interpreted by computers.

Then the LLM can perform its exactly same core text prediction algorithm on clean regularized universalized semantics freed of its syntactic shell, i.e., how it manifests superficially in particular phrasings within a natural language and particular natural languages, and simply translate the EngLISP result back into the user’s chosen natural language.

Think of it as the Java bytecode for natural languages, to move past syntax into the underlying semantics, breaking the ceiling for LLMs that merely optimize syntactic correctness and incurring hallucinations and confabulations that are difficult to avoid since to the LLM that operates on syntactic surfaces but not the semantic content of languages, a well-formed statement that is semantically nonsensical or incorrect is just as valid to the model as the actually semantically correct statement; it might even prefer syntactically optimized garbage over what is semantically correct but less well-written.

EngLISP is hypothetically useful for other interlinguistic use-cases, such as a universal intermediate language to translate between all natural languages while preserving the underlying semantic content, implemented by writing a compiler from any natural language to and from EngLISP, yet first I will start with a compiler turning English into EngLISP and EngLISP into English as a proof of concept.

Hence, EngLISP is an intermediate language in two senses: intermediate between natural language and computer programs, and intermediate between one natural language and another.

You can see how EngLISP is the application of the MinimaLIST approach to the concrete problems of natural language processing. It is the minimally sufficient set of abstractions that condenses the semantic content possessed by all human natural languages, containing nothing that is arbitrarily or incidentally found in particular languages only. It is an abstraction, i.e., on a higher level of generality. Treat it as an interface with respect to natural languages so that one can operate on semantic content in front of this interface with its numerous possible syntactic implementations hidden behind the interface.

Warning: I invoke X-bar notation syntax trees and Lisp S-expressions repeatedly, but they are beyond the scope of this paper, and I do not want to repeat or paraphrase what others have said many times before me, so I would invite readers to get familiar with them through the usual sources so that the following makes sense.

The Lisp S-expression structure is not arbitrary: I chose it it is the simplest, most regular, and most universalizable way to express the X-bar notation syntax trees that all natural languages have in common, with an isomorphism between X-bar trees and Lisp S-expressions. Additionally, English written using formal Lisp rules and the proper metaprogramming “preprocessing” compiles as correct Lisp code, making it readable by humans and machines.

Lisp is the natural expression of syntax trees and it gives us many useful formalistic properties. It dissolves the convoluted grammatical rulebooks for natural languages down to something that is simpler than any human language but still expressive enough to say anything that can be said (Wittgenstein), without a drop of syntactic complexity or ambiguity remaining. As a Turing-complete programming language family, Lisp programs can compute everything that can be computed by any other Turing-equivalent programming language.

EngLISP thus reveals the deep formal homology between any language understandable by humans and any language which can be compiled or interpreted by a computer, just as Lisp S-expressions are a thin wrapper over lambda calculus, equivalently expressive as Turing computational models, and express the “code = data” homoiconicity that enables effortless metaprogramming, especially due to how regular Lisp’s syntax is compared to every other programming language whose arbitrarily particular syntactic rules prevent them from achieving homoiconicity.

Again, the relationship between X-bar trees in linguistics and Lisp S-expressions is isomorphic, but the relationship between EngLISP and well-formed English is a homologous relationship in that the former is one-to-one but the latter is a one-to-many relationship created through transformation, yet easily verified by seeing the many English sentences as different ways to write the same semantic content using different words and ordering. This means that instead of having each English sentence get interpreted into a distinct EngLISP code snippet, a true one-to-one relationship, I produce a coerced canonical format that mandatorily forces English sentences with the same semantic content despite surface-level syntactic variations down to the exact same Lisp S-expression each time.

Hypothetically, this could be done by converting raw English into X-bar notation syntax trees, manipulating them into a regular form that I will have to define strictly as a formal grammar, and then producing an S-expression which as a nested set of atomic pairs is exactly one-to-one and isomorphic with X-bar trees. When I say “canonical”, I mean that every unique semantic snippet of content should always have the same formal representation, and when I say “coerced”, I mean that my EngLISP interpreter will guarantee and enforce that every natural language plaintext input that possesses the same common underlying semantic content will be interpreted into the same X-bar syntax tree and hence the same S-expression in Lisp.

Note that Lisp is a functional programming language, meaning “function-first”, revolving around dialectical processes that call each other and especially stateless processes with no side effects and no global state in which functions produce results purely based on the results of the functions within its body, unlike most popular well-used programming languages which buy into the object-oriented programming paradigm, or at the very least the imperative programming style of C-based languages, making them “object-first” and revolving around creating and destroying objects, as well as accessing and changing the state of objects.

English is SVO (subject-verb-object), so we have to artificially turn English into VSO or “verb-first” (perhaps even “verb-centered” in terms of verbs being first-order citizens and nouns and others being second-order citizens) to produce the same ordering as is baked into Lisp, because functions in programming languages tend to correspond to verbs in human language anyways and objects with state tend to correspond to nouns in human languages. “Noun-first” paradigms which make nouns first-order citizens, just like with object-oriented programming languages, may make it somewhat more difficult to express highly dialectical thoughts without lapsing back into the mode of creating, destroying, accessing, and mutating objects with a type hierarchy.

I will add to and explain the profound consequences of this dichotomy in a future article.

Since the core ruleset for Lisp syntax is already as simplistic and barebones as possible, which grants us these nice aforementioned formal-scientific properties, and also serves as a “neutral” encoding between the “philosophies” of different human languages that choose very divergent syntactic representations, the formal grammar reduces to a tractable problem, which is for each English word with an indispensable meaning to have a formal definition, like “(define x …)”, and if it is a verb or a noun to define what parameters it takes and in which order, like the English verb “produce” taking first a subject doing the producing, the object being produced, and a few optional modifiers giving time, space, and other contexts encoded into natural languages. We could get something like: “(produce I (bagel plural) yesterday)” for “I produced bagels yesterday”. I could have rendered “I” as “1st-person” as well.

I plan on handling these issues by doing a lightweight minimalist metaprogramming extension of a standard Lisp dialect so that having that on board is all that is needed to write correct EngLISP that compiles without needing to do the heavy scaffolding to make EngLISP expressions compatible with base Lisp dialects, like Scheme, Common Lisp, or Clojure, because I outsourced that chore to myself. These dialects are, respectively, the minimalistic barebones educational version, the one that is popular and has libraries but is not as “pure” as Scheme, and the one that is in the Java ecosystem and compiles to Java bytecode that runs on the JVM. Lisp is very well-known for how powerful it is at metaprogramming as a consequence of its “code = data” invariant property, letting it manipulate itself as language just like any other kind of data.

The “happy path” of conventional well-formed English should not be that difficult to transform into Lisp, but it is all the edge cases that will prove challenging for the EngLISP interpreter. The 80–20 rule will probably assert itself so that 80% of EngLISP input cases take 20% of engineer time, money, or lines of code, whereas 20% of EngLISP input cases take 80% of engineer time, money, or lines of code.

I chose to call this idea “EngLISP” because it was a catchy pun between “English” and “Lisp”, but this is an arbitrary design choice since I know English the best and it is the current most popular language. It is conceivable to do this in any human natural language and still maintain the same expressiveness as in English, but this task will be simpler for more analytic or isolating languages. English does have conjugations and affixes that make things somewhat harder, but I think it is “good enough” considering the real world social properties of English, especially its ubiquity globally and recognition as the unspoken canonical language of academia.

Using the English lexicon as the default convention for code and data is a pragmatic move to ensure that EngLISP is as easy for Anglophones to read as possible. One could use a different lexicon or even variable_1, variable_2, but only using English lexicon maximizes the chance of being understood as natural language even though the underlying Lisp S-expressions are the same. This enforces the “intermediate language” property of EngLISP simultaneously being casually understandable as a natural language and rigorously able to be compiled as a programming language. However, the compiler will compile correct Lisp code anyways regardless of how the programmer names the code and data. Likewise, due to human fuzziness in cognition and higher tolerance for error relative to computers, many technically-malformed EngLISP examples will be totally understandable by English speakers while causing the computer’s compiler to throw an error, since the human will have commonsensical intuition to infer what exact semantic content the other side intended.

Incorrectly-written EngLISP may cause different human readers to attribute to it many slightly-off or even mutually-incompatible underlying semantic foundations, which produces the ambiguity that I designed EngLISP to minimize or ideally eliminate.

Yet this goal is only achievable when people use it properly by the standards I will define as a formal grammar, which is for humans, and eventually a full-on interpreter or compiler as a metaprogramming extension of existing Lisp family programming languages, which is for computers.

I hope to prove that an EngLISP computer program or code snippet that compiles correctly produces some formal proof-theoretic guarantees on how rigorously unambiguous and exact it is when read as a natural language text in the sense of linguists and philosophers.
